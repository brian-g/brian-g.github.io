Aquaria


You bought that brand spanking new HDTV and you just love to tweak it. You watch all of you DVD’s on it over and over since there ain’t no HDTV programming anywhere, ‘cause the broadcasters still don't believe anyone is buying the damn things. Then your wife tells you have to play with your kid instead of the TV and you wonder, how will I ever get the set (and the kid) broken in so I can get ISF to calibrate it? The solution, turn it in to an aquarium and entertain the kid at the same time.

Aquari is a DVD that allows you to choose from five different aquarium settings and four different soundtracks. Also included is an detailed catalog of the fish shown on the disc, thus allowing you to use it as a resource for planning a real aquarium. Each of the different aquariums are in a different setting, only one of which is a freshwater tank.

The five aquariums include ”Classic”, “Natural”, “Coral Reef”, “Exotic” and “Freshwater”. Each is stocked with different fish in different surrounding, sure to please all. Each tank has a setting that at allow you to put the video in a loop so that it starts over at the end. The Aquari DVD has a “close-up” mode that follows many of the fish around the tank and lets one examine them closer also optionally allowing looping playback. I found this close-up mode to my liking as the background changed more often to avoid “burn-in” of my monitor and let me see the stunning fish and fauna in incredible detail.

What more to say about this DVD. There is no story or acting to talk about (although the fish do put on much better performances than certain gladiatorial thespians) and no plot development. Just pure unadulterated visual stimuli.

The DVD is a DVD-18 with three aquariums on one side, two aquariums and DVD-ROM content on the other. The DVD-ROM content consists of screensavers and calendars for Microsoft Windows platforms. All of the “fishographies” are also copied for the DVD-ROM portion. The screensavers are rather interesting, because actual video clips are copied onto your system from the DVD-ROM (about 25MB each). My only real complaint with the DVD-ROM software is that it installs by default to C:\Aquari instead of the standard c:\Program Files\Aquaria and will not allow you to change the install directory. When are software vendors going to realize why Microsoft went through the trouble of creating the ‘Program Files’ directory? I have also received reports of this screensaver locking up machines.

Video:

All of the video is presented in anamorphic widescreen. The producers claim that it was digitally recorded. The video is excellent.

At first, I though that this is DVD made use of Pan and Scan on the fly. The packaging claims “You can view all the tanks and variations in total 16:9 widescreen, if you have a widescreen TV. With a standard TV you can view the disc in letterbox. The disc also plays full screen on standard TVs”. I switched my player back and forth between 16:9 mode and P&S mode and could find no difference in the picture. I believe that the disc does make use of P&S codes, but only to ensure that the DVD players doesn't perform letterbox down conversion, thus displaying the anamorphic video unaltered. This has the effect of making the fish a little shorter than they normally would be, but if you donut know the difference, it probably won't matter.

There isn't a speck of dirt or film grain apparent on any of the video. The video is incredible and very close to life-like. I expected more, which I'm sure is due to the fact that I expected the much more fine detail from the small fish. This is why I preferred the close-up mode, since I wasn't bothered by not being able to resolve the smaller fish.

Audio:

Two of the audio tracks are 5.1 musical selection best described as relaxation tunes. Waves and aquarium sound compose the other two tracks. The relaxation music features very good low extension on the LFE track and very ambient. The audio tracks aren't real flashy, but nor do they have to be.
